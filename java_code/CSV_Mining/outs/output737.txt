Roxburgh Castle from the Teviot Facsimile of a pencil drawing of Roxburgh Castle by G. Home Depicts the River Teviot with the ruins of Roxburgh Castle, Scottish Borders, in the background. In his unfinished Autobiography (1808), Scott lists the 'distant vestiges of Roxburgh Castle' among the sights which make Kelso 'the most beautiful, if not the most romantic village in Scotland'. Roxburgh Castle is a ruined 11th-century royal residence which repeatedly changed hands during the Wars of Independence. Scott relates the tale of its daring capture by Sir James Douglas (the 'Black Douglas') in 1313 in Tales of a Grandfather, First Series (1828). The entrance hall at Abbotsford boasts two cannonballs supposed to have been used in the siege of Roxburgh Castle in 1460. Sir Walter Scott described Kelso in his unfinished Autobiography (1808) as 'the most beautiful, if not the most romantic village in Scotland'. Scott spent many summers in Kelso at his uncle Captain Robert Scott's house of Rosebank, which Scott inherited in 1804. Baikie, J. The Charm of the Scott Country. London: A. & C.
